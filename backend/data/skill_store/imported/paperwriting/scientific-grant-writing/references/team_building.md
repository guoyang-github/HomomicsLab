# Team Building and Qualifications for Grant Proposals

## Overview

Reviewers assess whether the assembled team has the expertise, experience, and resources to successfully execute the proposed research. This reference covers strategies for presenting your team effectively.

## Team Composition Principles

### Complementary Expertise
- Cover all technical areas required by the project
- Include both senior guidance and hands-on execution
- Add specialized skills not available in PI's lab
- Demonstrate prior successful collaborations
- Show institutional diversity when appropriate

### Role Clarity
Each team member should have a clearly defined role:
- **PI**: Overall leadership, intellectual vision, major decisions
- **Co-I**: Substantial intellectual contribution to specific aims
- **Collaborator**: Specific expertise, resources, or data contributions
- **Consultant**: Advice on specific technical or clinical aspects
- **Trainees**: Students and postdocs executing day-to-day research

## Biosketch Strategy

### NIH Biosketch (2024 format)
- **Personal Statement**: Tailor to specific proposal, highlight relevant expertise
- **Positions and Honors**: Focus on most relevant positions
- **Contributions to Science**: 5 key discoveries with impact statements
- **Research Support**: Active and completed grants relevant to proposal

### NSF Biographical Sketch
- **Professional Preparation**: Education and postdoctoral training
- **Appointments**: Current and recent positions
- **Publications**: Most closely related and other significant works
- **Synergistic Activities**: Broader impacts and service

### Key Principles
- Highlight publications most relevant to proposed work
- Show trajectory and productivity
- Address career gaps or interruptions if needed
- Limit to required length strictly
- Use consistent formatting

## Letters of Support

### Content Requirements
- Specific description of collaborator's contribution
- Confirmation of resources, data, or access being provided
- Statement of commitment to the project
- Description of prior working relationship
- Signed and on institutional letterhead

### Common Mistakes
- Generic letters that could apply to any proposal
- Vague commitments without specifics
- Unsigned or improperly formatted letters
- Letters from collaborators without clear project roles
- Last-minute requests resulting in weak letters

## Multi-Institutional Teams

### Coordination Strategies
- Designate a lead institution and PI
- Define subaward budgets and milestones
- Establish communication protocols
- Plan for data sharing and management
- Address intellectual property considerations

### Presenting Collaboration
- Show prior joint publications or grants
- Explain why multiple institutions are necessary
- Describe facilities at each institution
- Demonstrate geographic or demographic diversity if relevant

## Junior Investigators and Trainees

### Mentoring Plans
- Specific mentoring activities and schedules
- Career development goals for trainees
- Training in responsible conduct of research
- Plans for diversity and inclusion

### For Career Awards (K awards, CAREER)
- Integration of research and mentoring
- Independence from mentor's research program
- Plans for transitioning to independent funding
- Institutional commitment to career development

## Evaluating Team Strength

### Self-Assessment Checklist
- [ ] Does the team cover all required expertise areas?
- [ ] Are roles clearly defined and appropriate?
- [ ] Is there prior collaboration history?
- [ ] Do biosketches show relevant accomplishments?
- [ ] Are letters of support specific and strong?
- [ ] Is the team size appropriate for the budget?
- [ ] Are trainees included with adequate mentoring plans?
- [ ] Does the team demonstrate diversity?
