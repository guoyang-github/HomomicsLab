# Resubmission Strategies for Grant Proposals

## Overview

Most successful researchers have faced rejection. The key to eventual funding is learning from reviewer feedback and crafting a compelling revision. This reference covers strategies for turning criticism into a stronger proposal.

## NIH Resubmission (A1)

### The Introduction to Resubmission (1 page)

This is your opportunity to directly address reviewer concerns. Structure it as:

1. **Summary Paragraph**: Brief overview of changes
2. **Point-by-Point Responses**: Address each major criticism
3. **Substantial Improvements**: Highlight new data or major changes

### Writing Effective Responses

**Format**:
- Quote the reviewer's comment verbatim
- Provide a direct, respectful response
- Describe specific changes made
- Reference page/line numbers where changes appear

**Tone**:
- Be respectful and appreciative of reviewer efforts
- Never argue or dismiss criticism
- If you disagree, explain your reasoning politely
- Show that you take feedback seriously

**Examples of Good Responses**:

> "The reviewers noted that the sample size calculations were insufficient. We have now conducted formal power analyses for all aims (see Approach, p. 8, lines 120-135). Based on effect sizes from our preliminary data, we determined that n=45 per group provides 80% power to detect the primary outcome with alpha=0.05."

> "Reviewers raised concerns about the lack of alternative approaches. We have added a new section on contingency planning (p. 15, lines 280-310) that describes alternative methods for Aim 2 if the primary approach encounters technical difficulties."

### Strategies for Major Changes

**Adding Preliminary Data**
- New experiments directly addressing reviewer concerns
- Pilot studies demonstrating feasibility
- Additional validation data

**Revising Aims**
- Consider dropping problematic aims
- Reorder aims to address feasibility concerns
- Add new aims that strengthen overall project

**Changing Methods**
- Adopt reviewer-suggested approaches when appropriate
- Provide stronger justification for methods you're keeping
- Add expert collaborators for techniques outside your expertise

### When NOT to Resubmit

- Fundamental conceptual flaws in the hypothesis
- Lack of innovation or significance
- Missing critical expertise or resources
- More than 50% of aims need complete replacement
- Preliminary data contradict proposed approach

## NSF Resubmission

### NSF Revision Process
- No formal "Introduction to Resubmission" required
- Address reviewer concerns in revised narrative
- May be reviewed by same or different panel
- Program officer feedback is valuable

### Strategies
- Revise based on panel summary and PO feedback
- Consider changing target program if misalignment was noted
- Strengthen broader impacts if that was a weakness
- Add new preliminary data if feasibility was questioned
- Ensure all required sections are complete

### Timing Considerations
- May need to wait for next submission cycle
- Use time to generate new data or refine approach
- Consider whether a new submission is better than revision

## General Resubmission Principles

### Timeline for Revision
- **1-2 weeks**: Process emotions, read reviews carefully
- **2-4 weeks**: Develop revision strategy, plan new experiments
- **1-3 months**: Generate new preliminary data if needed
- **1-2 months**: Write revised proposal
- **2-4 weeks**: Internal review and feedback

### Getting External Feedback
- Share reviews with mentors and colleagues
- Conduct mock review sessions
- Ask someone who hasn't seen the proposal to read revision
- Consider hiring a grant consultant for high-stakes resubmissions

### Common Revision Mistakes
1. **Ignoring criticism**: Making only cosmetic changes
2. **Overcorrecting**: Abandoning good ideas due to one reviewer's opinion
3. **Defensive tone**: Arguing with reviewers in the response
4. **Incomplete responses**: Addressing some but not all concerns
5. **New weaknesses**: Introducing problems while fixing others
6. **Missing the big picture**: Fixing details but not overall impact

### Emotional Resilience
- Rejection is normal and expected
- Study section paylines are often 10-20%
- Many funded proposals were resubmissions
- Use feedback to improve, not to criticize yourself
- Talk to mentors who have faced similar rejections

## Tracking and Organizing Feedback

### Review Summary Matrix

| Reviewer | Strengths | Weaknesses | Concerns | Your Response |
|----------|-----------|------------|----------|---------------|
| R1 | Significance, team | Methods vague | Feasibility | Added detail p.8 |
| R2 | Innovation | No alternatives | Risk too high | Added backups p.15 |
| R3 | Approach | Significance weak | Not important | Strengthened p.3 |

### Priority Matrix
- **High Priority**: Issues raised by multiple reviewers
- **Medium Priority**: Issues that affect overall impact score
- **Low Priority**: Minor suggestions or single reviewer comments

## Success Rates

### NIH
- A0 (first submission): ~15-20% success rate
- A1 (first resubmission): ~30-35% success rate
- A2 (second resubmission): ~25-30% success rate

### NSF
- First submission: ~20-25% success rate
- Resubmission: ~30-40% success rate (varies by program)

### Key Insight
Resubmissions often have higher success rates than first submissions because reviewers see that you have responded to feedback and the proposal is stronger.
